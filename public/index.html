My model of COVID-19 risk

In college, I wasn't expecting to like my Probability class. The professor was kind of goofy. But I really liked how we were able to predict things more accurately than by using intuition. The birthday problem is perhaps the most memorable of these things. We also talked about some space disasters and how, if you did the math correctly, it would have been less surprising.

The main insight that I gained is that, even when a particular occurence is very unlikely, when you put it in a group the probability might go up much faster than you expect. Applied to our current situation, even though it's very unlikely that you'll run into an individual person with COVID-19, you would need a surprisingly small number of people in the same place to have at least one person who's infected.

This page has a model where you can choose a locality and a risk tolerance and see the largest group size you should be a part of. Remember that it's just a model. It's most useful in giving you a picture of how much different the risk level is between, say, a group of 10 and a group of 100. It's not scientifically rigorous. It's how my very dusty memory of probability is processing things today. It also deals with probabilities, which are much better at predicting things across an entire population than for a particular individual.

The formula I'm using is Pn = 1 - (1 - p)n. p is the probability that a random individual you meet is infected. n is the group size. Pn is the probability that someone in a group of n is infected.

Coming up with p is a challenge. I've chosen to make it i N-1, where N is the population and i is a guess at the number of people who are contagious and still circulating. I have set i = C c7, where c7 is the number of new cases in the last week. My assumption here is that the circulating contagious people are mostly those who are about to develop symptoms, and that anyone who has tested positive is quarantined. C = 2, to account for undetected cases.

Returning to normal

As things open back up and we go back to "normal", I've been trying to figure out how likely it is that there will be infected people at any particular gathering. This is most important for places where we spend a bunch of time or talk to a bunch of people.

Everything we do is a balance between risk and reward. COVID-19 has changed the level of risk, which means we might not choose to do all of the same things we chose to do in the past.

This page is my attempt to explore the risk that COVID-19 introduces to gatherings. It's not perfect, but it's how I'm working through this.

To me, the ideal scenario would have been that, in March, the US stayed at home and reduced the rate of infection to the point where, now, we'd be able to go back to doing almost everything with really minimal risk. Let's call that a squished curve. A do nothing approach would have been an uncontrolled exponential curve, which would have been a disaster. What we did flattened the curve, but it's made it so that the disease will be around for much longer. I anticipate the second half of 2020 will see a split between countries with squished curves and flattened curves, and much less commerce between those two sets of countries.
